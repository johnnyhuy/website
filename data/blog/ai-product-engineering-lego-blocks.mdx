The technology landscape has reached unprecedented competitive intensity. [Microsoft](https://www.microsoft.com/), [Google](https://www.google.com/), and [Meta](https://www.meta.com/) launch competing products monthly rather than yearly. In this environment, the fundamental question facing product teams isn't about technical implementation or organisational structure — it's answering "What do I build and why will it win?"

This question has become exponentially harder as competitive moats that once lasted six to twelve months now barely survive two to three weeks. The answer lies in treating AI like a series of Lego blocks where competitive advantage emerges from assembling differentiated products using your unique assets rather than the AI capabilities themselves.

## The Lego Blocks Framework

AI product engineering operates on a three-layer architecture where competitive differentiation comes from the unique assembly of standardised components:

```
AI Capabilities (commodity) -----
                                 \
Your Data (differentiation) -------> Differentiated Product -> Unique Output
                                 /
Your Functionality (control) -----
```

### AI Capabilities: The Commodity Layer

These are the standardised building blocks everyone has access to: pre-trained models from [OpenAI](https://openai.com/), [Anthropic](https://www.anthropic.com/), or [Google](https://www.google.com/); transcription services like [Deepgram](https://deepgram.com/); and various processing capabilities for audio, imaging, and text. Since these capabilities are universally available, they cannot provide sustainable competitive advantage.

### Your Data: The Differentiation Layer

Proprietary data provides the context that enables AI models to generate unique outputs. The competitive value depends on marginal advantage over what's already trained in foundation models. Critical data types include:

- **Real-time data** unavailable in training sets
- **User-specific behavioural data**
- **Domain-specific expertise** (legal, healthcare, finance)
- **Human judgement data** around curation and quality
- **Reinforcement data** from user interactions

**The marginal value of your data matters more than volume.** Ask: "How much additional value does our data provide over what's already in foundation models?"

### Your Functionality: The Control Layer

This determines how AI behaves within your product ecosystem and provides the "superpowers" that enable unique user experiences. Key components include:

- **Specialised workflows** tailored to user needs
- **Business rules** and logic specific to your domain
- **Integration capabilities** with existing tools and systems
- **User interface patterns** that control AI interaction

## The Granola Case Study: Finding a Seam in Saturated Markets

[Granola](https://www.granola.ai/) entered the AI note-taking market dominated by established players like [Otter](https://otter.ai/), [Fathom](https://fathom.video/), [Fireflies](https://fireflies.ai/), and incumbent platforms like [Zoom](https://zoom.us/) and [Teams](https://www.microsoft.com/en-us/microsoft-teams/group-chat-software). Despite this crowded landscape, they secured 40% awareness among technology professionals and AUD $30 million in funding within 12 months.

### Unmet Customer Need

While competitors focused on **replacing** the note-taking job entirely ("we'll take your meeting notes"), Granola identified an unmet need: users wanted to be **empowered** to take better notes, not have the task removed entirely. This insight revealed a significant gap between automated transcription and human-guided documentation.

### Data Flywheel

Granola's data strategy combines:
- **User-generated notes** during meetings
- **AI-enhanced transcription** from system audio
- **Contextual metadata** from calendar integrations
- **Incremental improvement data** from user edits and preferences

This creates a unique repository that improves over time, enabling downstream features like cross-meeting search, project workspaces, and automated actions.

### Functionality Differentiation

Granola's product controls include:
- **Guided note capture** rather than full automation
- **Human-in-the-loop refinement** with AI suggestions
- **Cross-meeting intelligence** that connects related discussions
- **Action item extraction** tied to calendar and task systems

## Two Failure Modes to Avoid

| Failure Mode | Symptom | Why It Happens |
|-------------|---------|---------------|
| **Reinvention trap** | Building custom models when off-the-shelf works | Engineering pride, NIH syndrome, underestimating foundation model capabilities |
| **Copy-paste trap** | Assembling identical Lego stacks as competitors | No unique data or functionality, competing purely on UI polish |

The reinvention trap wastes engineering cycles on problems already solved. The copy-paste trap creates undifferentiated products that compete on price alone.

## Moat Sequencing Timeline

```
Month 0-1:  Launch with unique data + standard AI (temporary feature advantage)
Month 2-3:  Competitors copy features (moat compresses)
Month 3-6:  Data flywheel creates marginal improvement (defensible if data is unique)
Month 6-12: Workflow integration deepens (switching costs emerge)
Month 12+:  Platform effects from user-generated content (network moat)
```

Each layer of moat depends on the previous one. Without unique data, workflow integration is just UI preference. Without workflow integration, network effects never materialise.

## Summary

- **AI capabilities are commodities.** Everyone has access to the same models and APIs.
- **Your data is the first differentiation layer.** Focus on marginal value over foundation models, not volume.
- **Your functionality is the control layer.** Specialised workflows and integration create switching costs.
- **Avoid the reinvention trap.** Don't build what you can buy. Don't build what foundation models already do.
- **Avoid the copy-paste trap.** If your Lego assembly is identical to competitors, you're competing on price.

## What's Next

Audit your current product: what percentage of your AI differentiation comes from unique data and workflows versus UI polish on commodity capabilities? If the answer skews toward polish, your moat is thinner than you think.
