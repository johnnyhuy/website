Your colleagues are happy ticking Jira tickets and clocking out at 5 PM. Nothing wrong with that. Valid choice.

But when your ambition outpaces your environment, the friction gets exhausting unless you have a strategy.

I've been in teams where the median engineer had no interest in growth. Here's what actually works.

I spent two years on a platform team where the standing joke was "why would you learn that? It won't change your pay grade." The senior engineers were competent, but they'd stopped learning five years ago. Team brown bags died from lack of attendance. Conferences required begging. When I suggested we try infrastructure-as-code instead of clicking through AWS consoles, one senior dev told me it sounded like "more work for the same outcome."

That environment could have flattened me. Instead, I got deliberate about growth.

## Build Your Own Career Pyramid

Steve McConnell's career pyramid idea: define what you're building toward, or your current environment becomes your ceiling.

**Know what you want.** What skills? What impact? What's the long-term goal? A clear vision keeps you motivated when the day-to-day feels like a treadmill.

**Pick projects that compound.** Avoid "lily pad hopping" — jumping between disconnected tasks with no cumulative value. Each project should build on the last.

I learned this the hard way. Early in my career I said yes to everything: a bit of frontend here, some database tuning there, a DevOps ticket, a API migration. After 18 months I could list a dozen technologies on my resume but couldn't go deep on any of them. In interviews I'd describe breadth but couldn't speak to hard-won expertise. It looked busy but it was career noise.

The shift came when I started asking: "what will this teach me that my last project didn't?" I picked the migration to containerised deployments because I'd already done virtual machines. I volunteered for the API gateway project because I'd built REST services and wanted to understand traffic management. Each piece stacked.

| Approach | Result After 2 Years |
|----------|---------------------|
| Lily pad hopping | Broad but shallow, no clear expertise |
| Pyramid building | Deep capability in 2-3 areas, clear trajectory |

A colleague of mine, let's call him Dave, did the opposite. Dave was brilliant but scattered. He'd fix a bug in the payment service, then volunteer for a mobile feature, then pick up a data pipeline ticket. Each task got done well, but after three years Dave couldn't point to a domain he owned. When promotion cycles came around, the engineers who'd gone deep on one or two areas had clear narratives. Dave had a list. Lists don't get you to senior.

## Create Your Own Opportunities

When opportunities aren't handed to you, make them.

**Fill the gaps.** Look for unresolved problems or places where new tech could help. Step up. The projects nobody wants are often the ones that teach you the most.

**Keep learning.** I spend 5-10 hours a week on structured learning outside work. Courses, reading, meetups. Expands your value, opens new paths.

**Find mentors.** Inside and outside your org. Some of my most valuable career conversations happened over coffee with people I met at conferences. They see things your immediate peer group doesn't.

Creating opportunities when nobody's handing them out looks different than the LinkedIn version. It's not about starting a side project that gets 10,000 GitHub stars. It's quieter than that.

For me it meant noticing that our deployment process broke every second release, then spending two weekends building a prototype pipeline that cut deploy time from 45 minutes to 12. I didn't ask permission. I didn't wait for a ticket. I built it, demoed it to my manager, and asked if we could trial it. That project became the basis for how the whole division deployed software eighteen months later. But it started because I was bored on a Saturday and saw something broken.

Another time I realised nobody understood our observability stack. The team was paying for Datadog but using 10% of it. I spent a month learning it properly, set up proper dashboards and alerts, then ran a lunch-and-learn for the broader engineering group. I wasn't the most senior person in the room. I was just the person who'd done the work. That visibility got me pulled into architecture conversations I'd never have been invited to otherwise.

The pattern is: find a gap that matters, fill it before anyone asks, then make sure the right people see it. Not in a performative way. Just: here's a thing that was broken, now it's less broken, let me show you how.

## Influence Without Preaching

Your attitude can spark curiosity without direct persuasion.

**Ship good work consistently.** Share interesting articles. Show genuine enthusiasm. I've watched quiet engineers start asking questions after seeing someone else ship something genuinely useful.

**Offer help collaboratively.** See a colleague struggling? Spot a process that could improve? Offer support without being judgmental. Builds goodwill, gradually shifts culture.

I've watched this work slowly but genuinely. On that platform team with the "why learn that?" attitude, there was a mid-level engineer, Sarah, who never spoke in meetings. Never pushed back on bad ideas. Just took tickets and delivered them. I started sitting next to her and asking questions about her work. Not in a patronising way — I was genuinely curious about the reporting module she'd built. Over a few months she started offering opinions in standups. Then she suggested a refactor. Six months later she was leading technical decisions on her stream.

She didn't need a motivational speech. She needed to see that enthusiasm wasn't dangerous. That shipping good work got noticed. I didn't convert the whole team, but one person raising their ambition changes the room's energy more than you'd expect.

But here's the catch: this only works if the organisation has *any* appetite for improvement. Sometimes the walls are too thick.

## Know When to Leave

Sometimes the culture is fundamentally indifferent to growth. I've seen talented engineers waste years trying to change cultures that were never going to change.

The signals aren't subtle once you know to look for them. When promotion criteria are opaque and always seem to favour the same people. When your manager changes every six months and nobody remembers your goals. When you suggest a new approach and the response is "we've always done it this way" — not because the old way is good, but because change is effort. When the senior engineers are coasting and the organisation rewards tenure over impact.

I stayed too long at one company because the pay was comfortable and the work was easy. Two years passed. When I finally interviewed elsewhere, I realised how far behind my skills had drifted. Not dramatically — I could still do the job — but I'd missed entire waves of tooling and practice. The market had moved. I had to play catch-up that could have been avoided.

**Assess honestly.** Does leadership care about development? If not, you're fighting uphill. Do people get promoted for impact or for longevity? Are the senior engineers still learning, or are they curators of outdated expertise?

**Find compatible culture.** In interviews, ask about learning opportunities, career progression, team dynamics. Find somewhere your drive is valued. I now ask every hiring manager: "tell me about the last person on your team who grew into a more senior role. What did that path look like?" The quality of that answer tells me everything. If they can't name someone, or the story is "they were just ready," that's a warning. If they light up and describe specific projects, mentorship, and sponsorship, that's where I want to be.

> [!TIP] The 80/20 Rule for Career Growth
> Focus on the 20% of activities yielding 80% of your growth. High-impact learning, strategic networking, challenging projects. Not routine tasks.

## Summary

- **Define your career pyramid.** Know what you're building toward.
- **Create your own opportunities.** Don't wait for growth to be handed to you.
- **Avoid lily pad hopping.** Choose projects that compound.
- **Influence through shipping.** Not preaching.
- **Know when to leave.** Some cultures can't be changed from within.

## What's Next

Next time you feel your environment limiting you, ask: am I waiting for permission, or creating my own momentum? That difference determines whether you stagnate or accelerate.
