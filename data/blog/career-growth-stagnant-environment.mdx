Your colleagues are happy ticking Jira tickets and clocking out at 5 PM. Nothing wrong with that. Valid choice.

But when your ambition outpaces your environment, the friction gets exhausting unless you have a strategy.

I've been in teams where the median engineer had no interest in growth. Here's what actually works.

## Build Your Own Career Pyramid

Steve McConnell's career pyramid idea: define what you're building toward, or your current environment becomes your ceiling.

**Know what you want.** What skills? What impact? What's the long-term goal? A clear vision keeps you motivated when the day-to-day feels like a treadmill.

**Pick projects that compound.** Avoid "lily pad hopping" — jumping between disconnected tasks with no cumulative value. Each project should build on the last.

| Approach | Result After 2 Years |
|----------|---------------------|
| Lily pad hopping | Broad but shallow, no clear expertise |
| Pyramid building | Deep capability in 2-3 areas, clear trajectory |

## Create Your Own Opportunities

When opportunities aren't handed to you, make them.

**Fill the gaps.** Look for unresolved problems or places where new tech could help. Step up. The projects nobody wants are often the ones that teach you the most.

**Keep learning.** I spend 5-10 hours a week on structured learning outside work. Courses, reading, meetups. Expands your value, opens new paths.

**Find mentors.** Inside and outside your org. Some of my most valuable career conversations happened over coffee with people I met at conferences. They see things your immediate peer group doesn't.

## Influence Without Preaching

Your attitude can spark curiosity without direct persuasion.

**Ship good work consistently.** Share interesting articles. Show genuine enthusiasm. I've watched quiet engineers start asking questions after seeing someone else ship something genuinely useful.

**Offer help collaboratively.** See a colleague struggling? Spot a process that could improve? Offer support without being judgmental. Builds goodwill, gradually shifts culture.

## Know When to Leave

Sometimes the culture is fundamentally indifferent to growth. I've seen talented engineers waste years trying to change cultures that were never going to change.

**Assess honestly.** Does leadership care about development? If not, you're fighting uphill.

**Find compatible culture.** In interviews, ask about learning opportunities, career progression, team dynamics. Find somewhere your drive is valued.

> [!TIP] The 80/20 Rule for Career Growth
> Focus on the 20% of activities yielding 80% of your growth. High-impact learning, strategic networking, challenging projects. Not routine tasks.

## Summary

- **Define your career pyramid.** Know what you're building toward.
- **Create your own opportunities.** Don't wait for growth to be handed to you.
- **Avoid lily pad hopping.** Choose projects that compound.
- **Influence through shipping.** Not preaching.
- **Know when to leave.** Some cultures can't be changed from within.

## What's Next

Next time you feel your environment limiting you, ask: am I waiting for permission, or creating my own momentum? That difference determines whether you stagnate or accelerate.
