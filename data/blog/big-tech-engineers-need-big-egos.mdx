You need ego to work in big tech. Not the loud kind. The quiet kind that says "I can figure this out" even when the evidence says you can't.

## Why Ego Matters

Large-scale engineering is humbling. You're wrong most of the time. The codebase is too big to understand. You still have to move forward.

That quiet ego is what keeps you investigating when nothing makes sense. It's what lets you take a position in a room full of senior people who disagree. It's what stops you from hedging forever.

I learned this during a three-week outage investigation. A payment processing system was failing intermittently. Seven engineers had looked at it. Everyone had a theory. None of them matched the data. The team lead wanted to rollback the last three deployments and hope for the best.

I didn't have a better theory. But I had a strong hunch that the rollback wouldn't help. The failure pattern didn't match deployment times. I said so in the incident channel. Got pushback from two senior engineers who were convinced it was a recent change. I held my position — not because I was sure I was right, but because I was sure the rollback was wrong. We kept investigating. Six hours later we found a race condition in a library that had been in production for eight months. The recent deployments were a red herring.

If I'd stayed quiet, we'd have rolled back, seen no improvement, and spent another day chasing our tails. My ego — my belief that I could see something the room was missing — saved us that day. It wasn't arrogance. It was the willingness to be wrong in public rather than let a worse decision happen by default.

| Situation | Low-ego Move | Useful Ego Move |
|-----------|-------------|-----------------|
| Confusing system behaviour | Freeze up, wait for permission | Keep digging until the problem reveals itself |
| Architecture debate | "Both options have pros and cons" | Take a position, name the trade-offs |
| Bad assumption in a meeting | Stay silent | Correct it clearly and early |
| Org-wide change | Avoid the conflict | Push through knowing some people will hate it |

## Big Tech Needs It Even More

In large orgs, decisions don't make themselves. Someone has to fill the vacuum or a less-informed person will.

Three behaviours that matter:

1. **Take positions.** Endless nuance just lets worse decisions happen.
2. **Tolerate making enemies.** Useful change usually annoys someone.
3. **Interrupt bad reasoning early.** Silence lets bad assumptions turn into funded work.

Sometimes you're the only person in the room seeing the problem clearly. You need enough ego to believe that might be true.

## The Counterweight: Organisational Humility

Same engineer, different context. When exec priorities shift, you need to drop the ego fast.

You can be right, do excellent work, and still lose because of politics three levels up. Projects get canned. Priorities reverse. Strong effort goes unrewarded.

I spent four months leading a migration to a new service mesh. Built the roadmap. Got buy-in from three teams. Wrote the RFC. Started the implementation. Two weeks before the first production rollout, the VP of Engineering decided we were standardising on a different vendor's solution. The decision came from a partnership negotiation I had no visibility into. My work wasn't wrong. It just wasn't the company's priority anymore.

The first time this happened to me, I took it personally. I was angry. I felt disrespected. I vented to my manager about the wasted effort. He listened patiently, then said: "Johnny, you built the right thing at the wrong time. The company doesn't owe you a victory lap for that." He was right. I'd confused technical correctness with organisational alignment. They are not the same thing.

The skill is absorbing that without treating every setback as a personal injustice.

```
High ego in technical work
    -> Strong positions -> Drives decisions
        -> Execs align? -> Project advances
        -> Execs don't align? -> Project dies
            -> Low ego response -> Don't take it personally -> Move on
```

We're paid to execute, not to unilaterally set strategy. Separating those two is how you avoid burnout.

I've watched engineers burn out because they couldn't make this separation. They'd fight every priority shift. They'd argue with directors about strategy. They'd treat every cancelled project as a betrayal. Those engineers were often technically excellent. But they exhausted themselves trying to win battles that weren't theirs to fight.

The ones who lasted? They had a simple rule: fight hard for technical decisions within your scope. Accept strategic decisions outside your scope. Know which is which.

## The Chameleon Model

Effective staff engineers aren't always humble or always forceful. They're adaptive.

High ego when ambiguity or weak reasoning needs pushing. Low ego when the company makes a strategic call you don't control.

I watched a staff engineer, let's call her Priya, navigate this perfectly. In a technical review, she tore apart a proposed architecture. Pointed out the single points of failure. Questioned the cost projections. Pushed back on the timeline. She was forceful, direct, unapologetic. The room listened. The architecture changed.

Two weeks later, the same VP who'd cancelled my service mesh project announced a reorg that moved her team under a different director. Priya's first question in the team meeting wasn't "why wasn't I consulted?" It was "what does this change about our priorities, and how do I help the team transition?" No resentment. No performative disagreement. Just practical adaptation.

That's the chameleon. She could be the most forceful person in the room when the topic was technical. And the most cooperative person in the room when the topic was organisational. She knew which layer she was operating in.

Not everyone gets this right. I've seen two failure modes repeatedly:

**The permanently humble engineer.** Always agreeable. Always accommodating. Never takes a position that might upset someone. These engineers are lovely to work with. They're also invisible in promotion cycles. When leadership discusses who to advance, nobody remembers them because they never stood for anything. They become professional yes-people. Nice, competent, and stuck.

**The permanently forceful engineer.** Always has a position. Always pushes back. Always corrects. These engineers get labelled as difficult. As "not a team player." As someone who "doesn't know how to pick their battles." Eventually they're managed out or sidelined. The org doesn't crush them dramatically. It just stops inviting them to important conversations.

- Too soft? Nothing gets decided.
- Too proud? The org eventually crushes you.
- The middle ground is psychologically demanding — confidence without entitlement.

That's why strong staff engineers are rare. The switching is exhausting. You have to believe you're right enough to argue. And believe you might be wrong enough to let go. Both at the same time.

## Summary

- **Big codebases need stabilising ego.** You'll be wrong and confused. Belief that you can still find the answer is the useful kind.
- **Big tech rewards commitment.** Take positions, tolerate friction, interrupt bad reasoning.
- **Org humility is the counterweight.** Strategy isn't your layer. Don't personalise executive reversals.
- **Effective staff engineers are chameleons.** High ego with peers, low ego with executives.

## What's Next

Next time you feel hesitation in a technical debate, ask: is my silence helping the project, or just protecting my comfort? The answer usually tells you whether you need more ego in that room, not less.
